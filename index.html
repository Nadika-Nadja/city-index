The Score Formula

8 dimensions scored 1–10: Transport · Public Space · Cleanliness · Cost vs Life · Economy (average of Scale + Resilience) · Leisure · Openness · Heritage. Ecology scored separately at half weight. Total = ((8 × base) + (0.5 × ecology)) / 8.5.

Cost vs Life is PPP-adjusted — median rent as a fraction of median wage in local purchasing power. A Mumbai flat at ₹65,000/month and a Bogotá apartment at COP 2,500,000/month are compared on what fraction of median local wages each represents, not absolute currency conversion.

🌿 Ecology at Half Weight — Why

Ecology is scored on present reality — residents breathe present air, drink present water. But ecology's causes are not historically uniform, and the score should be read accordingly.

The Thames was biologically dead in 1957. The Cuyahoga river in Cleveland caught fire in 1969. Every currently "clean" Western city followed the same sequence: industrialise, accumulate wealth, remediate. That sequence was not available to post-colonial cities in the same way. British colonial policy in India was not ecologically neutral — canal irrigation for export agriculture drew down rivers, deforestation for railway sleepers stripped catchments, urban infrastructure was designed for colonial extraction not liveable urbanism.

Singapore in 1965 had indicators comparable to Colombo or Penang. Its trajectory was enabled by city-state governance and geopolitical positioning unavailable to most post-colonial cities. None of this changes the score. A Delhi resident breathes the air that exists, not the air that history explains. But present reality has causes, and those causes matter for understanding what improvement requires.

On Openness — Global Calibration

Openness measures women's safety and mobility, LGBTQ+ legal rights and daily experience, minority community integration, press freedom, and social mixing across class and background. Amsterdam (9.5) and Berlin (9.0) set the top end. Chinese cities (Shanghai 3.5, Beijing 3.0) sit at the lower end — the Great Firewall, surveillance infrastructure (world's most extensive), 2021 Pride ban, documented treatment of ethnic minorities. Both the physical city excellence and the political constraints are real. Neither cancels the other.

Paris scores 6.5 — not because France is intolerant in law, but because the banlieue reality, laïcité friction with Muslim communities, and documented police violence represent real gaps between the City of Lights brand and the lived experience of significant communities.

Heritage — Correcting Bias

Heritage is corrected for systematic bias in standard rankings that overweight Mughal-era monument aesthetics and photogenic tourism sites. This index weights civilisational continuity (how long continuously inhabited), living heritage (tradition still practiced, not just preserved), and cultural footprint (did this culture influence regions beyond itself).

Rome scores 10 — the only city in this index to do so. The Colosseum, Pantheon (still in use, 2,000 years old), Vatican, 900 churches — the depth is qualitatively different. Madurai's 9.0 reflects 2,500+ years of continuous habitation and living ritual practice. It is not ranked below Delhi's 9.0 because Dravidian heritage is less significant — it is differently significant.

Latin America — Colonial Infrastructure Debt

Mexico City's subsidence — the city physically sinking as an over-extracted lake bed aquifer collapses — is inseparable from the colonial destruction of Lake Texcoco that Aztec Tenochtitlán was built on. São Paulo's Tietê river and Bogotá's Bogotá River carry structural deficits of cities that grew rapidly under colonial resource extraction followed by debt-constrained post-independence governance.

Africa's ecological scores — Durban, Addis Ababa, Casablanca, Tripoli — reflect similar dynamics. Infrastructure deficits colonial economies created and debt structures that constrain post-colonial governments from addressing them are the honest context for reading those scores.

What This Index Doesn't Measure (Yet)

Governance trajectory — is the city improving or worsening? Direction of travel matters as much as current position. Portland's downtown is worse than 5 years ago; Berlin's ecology is better; Bogotá's safety is dramatically improved.

Climate vulnerability — sea level rise risk for Mumbai, Chennai, Amsterdam, NYC, Shanghai, and Miami differs qualitatively from inland cities. This is not captured in ecology scoring.

Social mobility — can someone born poor in this city realistically improve their position? The gap between formal openness and actual mobility is the defining question for most cities in this index.

Food systems access — quality and affordability of food across income levels. Partially captured in cost vs life but deserves its own dimension. These are v7 candidates.

Marketing Gap explained: LOW GAP = city's promotion is largely honest · MED GAP = meaningful omissions in city promotion (common) · HIGH GAP = substantial divergence between the marketed city and the lived experience of most residents. High gap cities are not necessarily worse cities — they often have extraordinary strengths alongside systematically underreported weaknesses. Delhi's 9.0 heritage and 2.5 ecology coexist. SF's natural beauty and 3.0 cost vs life coexist. The gap score flags where the marketing narrative is doing the most work.

Scored Dimensions (equal weight)

Transport: network coverage, cost as % min wage, first/last mile, all-class access
Public Space: free access, walking distance, shade, class diversity of actual users
Cleanliness: visible hygiene, drain function, garbage in non-showcase areas — distinct from Ecology
Cost vs Life: PPP-adjusted median rent/wage ratio, public healthcare, food, schooling
Economy: Scale (size, depth, global reach) + Resilience (sector diversity, shock absorption)
Leisure: entertainment breadth and access across income levels
Openness: women's safety at night, minority experience, LGBTQ+ rights, press freedom, social mixing
Heritage: depth, civilisational continuity, living vs museum quality — bias-corrected

🌿 Ecology (half weight)

River/water body health · Groundwater trajectory · Green cover 10-year change · Annual PM2.5 burden · Government initiative quality measured by whether indicators are reversing, not by schemes announced

Formula

Total = ((8 × base_score) + (0.5 × ecology)) / 8.5
base_score = mean of 8 dimensions
Economy = (Scale + Resilience) / 2
