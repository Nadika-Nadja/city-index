South Asia (22 cities) alongside East Asia (Tokyo, Singapore, Seoul, Shanghai, Beijing, Chongqing) and Africa (Casablanca, Addis Ababa, Durban, Tripoli). Ecology is scored at half weight — it matters, but its causes are historically unequal. See methodology note below.

8 Scored Dimensions

Transport · Public Space · Cleanliness · Cost vs Life · Economy Scale · Economy Resilience · Leisure · Openness · Heritage. Economy feeds the score as the average of Scale and Resilience. All 8 dimensions weighted equally. Overall score = arithmetic mean.

Cost vs Life is PPP-adjusted — median rent as a fraction of median wage, in local purchasing power terms. A Tokyo apartment at ¥120,000/month and a Chennai apartment at ₹20,000/month are compared on what fraction of median local wages each represents, not absolute currency.

🌿 Ecology at Half Weight

Ecology is scored on present reality — residents breathe present air, drink present water. But ecology's causes are not historically uniform, and the score should be read accordingly.

The Thames was biologically dead in 1957. The Cuyahoga river in Cleveland caught fire in 1969. Every currently "clean" Western city followed the same sequence: industrialise, accumulate wealth, remediate. That sequence was not available to post-colonial cities.

British colonial policy in India was not ecologically neutral. Canal irrigation for export agriculture drew down river flows. Deforestation for railway sleepers stripped catchment areas that fed rivers Indian cities now sit on degraded versions of. Post-independence governments inherited infrastructure designed for colonial extraction, not liveable urbanism — and they inherited the fiscal constraints that came with it.

Singapore, Seoul, Tokyo — Context

Singapore in 1965 had indicators comparable to Colombo or Penang. Its subsequent ecological and infrastructural trajectory was enabled by city-state governance, concentrated fiscal resources, and a position in global trade that post-colonial cities were structurally excluded from accessing.

Tokyo's river restoration and Seoul's Cheonggyecheon stream recovery are real achievements — but they were made possible by the wealth generated by the same industrialisation that degraded those rivers first. The scores are honest; the context explains the work required to get there.

Africa — Colonial Infrastructure Debt

Durban, Addis Ababa, Casablanca, and Tripoli carry ecological and infrastructure stress that is inseparable from the deficits colonial economies created and the debt structures that constrain post-colonial governments from addressing them.

Tripoli's inclusion is deliberate. It documents what political failure and conflict cost in urban human terms. The scores are not a judgement on its residents — they are a documentation of what governance collapse means for a city with real heritage and real people.

On Heritage Scoring

Corrected for systematic bias in rankings that overweight Mughal-era monument aesthetics and photogenic tourism sites. This index weights civilisational continuity (how long continuously inhabited), living heritage (tradition still practiced, not just preserved), and geographic footprint (did this culture influence regions beyond itself).

Under these criteria, Dravidian temple urbanism, maritime Christian heritage, and syncretic court traditions score higher than pure monument count. Madurai's 9.0 reflects 2,500+ years of continuous habitation and living ritual practice. It is not ranked below Delhi's 9.0 because Dravidian heritage is less significant — it is differently significant.

China — Openness Scoring

Chinese cities (Shanghai, Beijing, Chongqing) score high on transport, cleanliness, and heritage — and low on openness. Both are honest. The Great Firewall, surveillance infrastructure, restrictions on assembly, and documented treatment of ethnic minorities are scored on the same basis as Ahmedabad's 2002 legacy or Lucknow's current political climate. The physical city and the political constraints coexist. Neither cancels the other.

What this index doesn't measure (yet): Governance trajectory (is the city improving or worsening — direction of travel matters as much as current position) · Climate vulnerability (sea level rise risk for Mumbai, Chennai, Shanghai differs qualitatively) · Social mobility (can someone born poor here realistically improve their position) · Food systems access across income levels. These are v6/v7 dimensions. Naming them as known omissions is more honest than pretending the index is complete.

Scored Dimensions (equal weight)

Transport: network coverage, cost as % min wage, first/last mile, all-class access
Public Space: free access, walking distance, shade, class diversity of actual users
Cleanliness: visible hygiene, drain function, garbage in non-showcase areas — distinct from Ecology
Cost vs Life: PPP-adjusted median rent/wage ratio, public healthcare, food, schooling
Economy Scale: size, depth, global connectivity
Economy Resilience: sector diversity, shock absorption, informal economy health
Leisure: entertainment breadth and access across income levels
Openness: women's safety at night, minority experience, caste/race texture, social mixing
Heritage: depth, civilisational continuity, living vs museum quality — bias-corrected

🌿 Ecology (half weight)

Sub-indicators: River/water body health (CPCB/equivalent monitoring, not declared quality) · Groundwater trajectory · Green cover 10-year change (satellite-derived) · Air quality annual PM2.5 burden · Government initiative quality (measured by whether indicators are reversing, not by schemes announced)
