🌦 Climate Dimension — v7a

Two sub-scores averaged to give the climate score:

Present Liveability (1–10): Based on monthly average high temperatures. Comfort band = 16–33°C avg high. Penalties: extreme heat months (avg high >38°C), cold months (avg high <7°C), humid-hot months (RH >75% AND avg high >28°C), wildfire smoke months, flood disruption. This is a year-round resident score, not a tourist score.

Trajectory (1–10): Direction of travel over 20 years. Sea level risk, groundwater stress, temperature trend, wildfire exposure, flood intensity trajectory. Higher = more stable or improving. This is qualitatively scored against IPCC regional projections and city-specific data.

Government policy scoring (v7b) deferred — policy exists separately from outcomes and deserves its own dimension.

Both ecology and climate feed the total at half weight. The 8 core liveability dimensions remain the dominant signal. A city with great infrastructure but deadly climate still ranks high on infrastructure — but the total correctly penalises the climate reality.

On Climate Scoring — What It Measures

Present liveability is not about "best time to visit." It asks: what is it like to live in this city year-round? Addis Ababa (2,355m elevation) has remarkable climate stability — 20–25°C all year, low humidity. That is a genuine quality of life advantage. Trichy (Tamil Nadu) has 4 months of 38–41°C peaks with 60–70% humidity. That is a genuine quality of life burden.

Smoke is explicitly scored. Portland, Seattle, SF, and LA face weeks of hazardous AQI annually from regional wildfires. This does not appear in temperature or humidity data — it requires a separate input.

Trajectory captures futures, not just presents. Edinburgh has an objectively grey climate (present: 4.5) but Scotland's renewable transition and minimal heat/flood risk give it an excellent trajectory (6.5). Delhi has a bearable winter and springs, but the trajectory (2.5) is the most alarming in the index — wet-bulb temperatures approaching survivability thresholds by 2050.

🌿 Ecology — Half Weight Context

Ecology and climate are related but distinct. Ecology scores the present health of rivers, groundwater, air quality (annual burden), and green cover trajectory. Climate scores weather patterns and their direction of change.

Delhi's AQI crisis appears in both — ecology captures the annual PM2.5 burden; climate captures the smoke months and trajectory. The double penalty is intentional: Delhi's air quality crisis is both an ecological failure and a climate-amplified seasonal disaster.

Colonial Context — Climate Trajectories

The worst climate trajectories in this index are concentrated in South Asia, coastal Africa, and Southeast Asia. This is not coincidence. Colonial infrastructure designed for extraction, not urban resilience. Post-independence debt structures constraining adaptation investment. And greenhouse emissions concentrated in wealthy nations causing outsized warming in tropical regions.

Addis Ababa's exceptional elevation-buffered climate exists alongside Ethiopia's severe water stress from climate-intensified drought cycles. The relationship between present climate advantage and colonial history is not simple — but the relationship between worst trajectories and post-colonial governance constraints is real.

What v7b Will Add

Government Climate Policy (1–10): Quality and ambition of response, not just existence. C40 membership and compliance, net zero targets and legislative backing, actual emissions trajectory (not pledged), adaptation spend and infrastructure, political durability (is policy reversible?), equity in adaptation.

Tokyo's mandatory city-level cap-and-trade (world's first, 2010) is categorically different from Delhi's Heat Action Plan that exists on paper with 5% coverage. That difference needs its own score.

Reading climate scores alongside city totals: A city can have an excellent overall score (Tokyo 8.4 v6) with a decent climate score (6.5) and still be experiencing measurable worsening. The trajectory sub-score is the number to watch — it tells you which direction the city is moving on climate, independent of where it starts.
