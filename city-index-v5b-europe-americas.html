8 Scored Dimensions + Ecology at ½ Weight

Transport · Public Space · Cleanliness · Cost vs Life · Economy Scale · Economy Resilience · Leisure · Openness · Heritage. Economy = average of Scale and Resilience. Ecology at half weight feeds the total: Total = ((8 × base) + (0.5 × ecology)) / 8.5.

Cost vs Life is PPP-adjusted: median rent as a fraction of median wage in local purchasing power terms. A London flat at £2,500/month and a Bogotá apartment at COP 2,500,000/month are compared on what fraction of median local wages each represents.

The Ecology Sequence — Thames, Cuyahoga, Seine

The Thames was biologically dead in 1957. The Cuyahoga river in Cleveland caught fire in 1969. Every currently clean Western city followed the same sequence: industrialise, accumulate wealth, remediate. Amsterdam's water management and London's Thames recovery are real — and they were funded by the wealth that industrialisation generated first. This does not exempt Western cities from ecological scoring. NYC scores 6.5, SF scores 7.5. The scores are honest. The context explains what made recovery possible.

Latin America — Colonial Infrastructure Debt

Mexico City's subsidence — the city physically sinking as an over-extracted lake bed aquifer collapses — is inseparable from the colonial destruction of Lake Texcoco that Aztec Tenochtitlán was built on. The drainage of the lake for colonial agriculture initiated a water management crisis that persists today. São Paulo's Tietê river and Bogotá's Bogotá River both carry structural deficits of cities that grew rapidly under colonial resource extraction followed by debt-constrained post-independence governance. The ecology scores reflect present reality. The context explains the work required.

On Openness — Global Calibration

Openness measures: women's safety and mobility, LGBTQ+ legal rights and daily experience, minority community integration, press freedom, and social mixing across class and background. Amsterdam (9.5) and Berlin (9.0) lead globally. Paris scores 6.5 — not because France is intolerant in law, but because the banlieue reality, laïcité friction with Muslim communities, and documented police violence represent real gaps. London scores 7.5 — genuinely cosmopolitan but class-stratified in ways that constrain the openness experienced by different income groups very differently.

The American Cost Crisis

San Francisco scores 3.0 on cost vs life — the worst in the entire 51-city index. The median 1BR rent of ~$3,200/month against median (not tech) wages is a ratio that is genuinely catastrophic for working residents. NYC scores 4.0, LA scores 4.5. Berlin (7.5) and Mexico City (7.5) score near the top globally on cost vs life — Berlin because it remains Europe's most affordable major city despite rapid rent rises; Mexico City because PPP-adjusted purchasing power for residents is extraordinary.

Heritage — Rome's 10 and NYC's 7

Rome scores 10 — the only city in the full 51-city index to do so. The Colosseum, Pantheon (still standing, still in use, 2,000 years old), Vatican, 900 churches — the depth is qualitatively different from everywhere else. NYC scores 7.0 on heritage — not because it lacks significance but because its heritage is recent, democratic, and disruptive: jazz, hip hop, abstract expressionism, Ellis Island. Both scores are correct. Heritage includes civilisational depth, architectural legacy, living tradition, and global cultural footprint.

What this index doesn't measure (yet): Governance trajectory — is the city improving or worsening? · Climate vulnerability — sea level rise risk differs qualitatively between Amsterdam, NYC, Miami, and inland cities · Social mobility — can someone born poor here realistically improve their position? · Food systems access across income levels. These are v6/v7 dimensions. Naming them as known omissions is more honest than pretending the index is complete.

🌿 Ecology Formula
